Intramuros,

the historic center of Manila and the political, educational, and religious hub of the Spanish Empire in the Philippines, was once a prosperous walled city ideally placed along Manila Bay. From the late 16th century through the early 19th century, it was the center of Spanish power. The walls were built between 1590 and 1872, covering the whole city of Manila at the time.

  • Pre-1571: The area now known as Intramuros was originally a settlement of indigenous peoples and Muslim traders. Establishment of Spanish Rule (1565-1600)
  • 1565: Miguel Lopez de Legazpi establishes a Spanish colony in Cebu. A ship under Martin de Goiti returns to Spain with a cargo of cinnamon; the possibility of opening between the Philippines and Spain is explored.
  • 1569: In search of better food supply, Legazpi transfers the colonists to Panay.
  • 1570: 8 May Spanish soldiers under Martin de Goiti sail from Panay to reconniter Manila Bay. The troop assaults Sulayman’s fortification and captures it for Spain.
  • 1571: April The Spaniards under Legazpi leave Panay to take control of Manila.
  • 1571: On May 24, Spanish conquistador Miguel López de Legazpi arrived in the Philippines and established the city of Manila. Intramuros, meaning "within the walls," was built as a fortress to protect the newly founded city.
  • 1595: Construction of Fort Santiago, a defensive fortress at the northern end of Intramuros, was completed. It would later serve as a military stronghold and prison during Spanish colonial rule.
  • 17th Century: Intramuros experienced significant growth, becoming the political, religious, and economic center of the Spanish East Indies.
  • 18th Century: Several religious structures were constructed within Intramuros, including the Manila Cathedral and San Agustin Church, which remain iconic landmarks to this day.
  • Late 19th Century: Intramuros underwent modernization efforts, including the construction of new buildings and improved infrastructure.
  • December 30, 1896: José Rizal, a prominent Filipino nationalist and writer, was executed by firing squad at Luneta (now Rizal Park) outside Intramuros. His death became a catalyst for the Philippine Revolution against Spanish rule.
  • 1898: During the Spanish-American War, the United States Navy under Commodore George Dewey defeated the Spanish fleet in Manila Bay. This led to the eventual transfer of sovereignty from Spain to the United States.
  • 1901: The Philippine Commission, appointed by the U.S. government, began planning efforts to rebuild Intramuros after its destruction during the war.
  • 1941: Japan invaded the Philippines during World War II. Intramuros, along with much of Manila, suffered heavy bombing and artillery attacks during the Battle of Manila.
  • 1945: The Battle of Manila concluded with the liberation of the city by American forces. Intramuros was left in ruins.
  • 1951: Reconstruction efforts of Intramuros began, aiming to restore some of the historic structures that were damaged or destroyed during World War II.
  • 1979: Intramuros was declared a National Historical Monument by the Philippine government.
  • 1980s and 1990s: The Intramuros Administration was established to oversee the restoration, maintenance, and promotion of the historic site.
  • 2000s: Efforts to preserve and restore Intramuros continued, focusing on maintaining its cultural heritage and promoting it as a tourist destination.

Rizal Park,

also known as Luneta Park, is a significant historical site dedicated to the national hero, Jose Rizal. It is where Rizal was executed by the Spanish colonial authorities in 1896.

  • 1861: The site of Rizal Park was originally designated as the Bagumbayan (Tagalog for "New Town"). It served as an execution ground during the Spanish colonial period.
  • December 30, 1896: José Rizal, a Filipino nationalist and advocate for reforms in the Philippines, was executed by firing squad at the Bagumbayan execution grounds. His death became a pivotal moment in the fight for Philippine independence from Spanish rule.
  • 1902: The Philippine Commission, under American colonial rule, renamed Bagumbayan to Luneta in honor of the first anniversary of Rizal's execution.
  • 1913: The Rizal Monument, designed by Swiss sculptor Richard Kissling, was erected at Luneta as a memorial to José Rizal. It stands as the park's centerpiece and a symbol of Philippine nationalism.
  • 1935: The inauguration of Rizal Park, also known as the Rizal Memorial Park, took place. It was declared a national park by the Philippine Legislature.
  • 1945: During World War II, Luneta and its surroundings were heavily damaged in the Battle of Manila. The park suffered significant destruction.
  • 1950: The Rizal Monument was restored after the damage caused by the war.
  • 1955: The park was officially renamed Rizal Park in honor of José Rizal's contributions to Philippine history and independence.
  • 1961: The centennial anniversary of José Rizal's birth was commemorated at Rizal Park with various events and celebrations.
  • 1982: Rizal Park underwent a major renovation in preparation for the visit of Pope John Paul II to the Philippines.
  • 1998: The Department of Tourism took over the administration of Rizal Park, leading to further improvements and developments.
  • 2015: Rizal Park was one of the main venues for the visit of Pope Francis during his apostolic journey to the Philippines.
  • Present: Rizal Park remains an important historical and cultural site in the Philippines, attracting locals and tourists alike. It is a place of reflection, leisure, and commemoration of the country's national hero and its struggle for independence.

Fort Santiago,

is a citadel located within Intramuros. It served as a defense fortress during the Spanish colonial period and witnessed various historical events, including the imprisonment and execution of national heroes.

  • Late 16th Century: Construction of Fort Santiago began during the Spanish colonial period, shortly after the establishment of the city of Manila in 1571. It was initially a simple palisade of bamboo and wood.
  • Late 16th to 17th Century: Fort Santiago underwent significant improvements and expansions under Spanish rule, transforming it into a stone fortress to defend against potential attacks from pirates, foreign invaders, and local rebels.
  • Late 1500s - 1762: Fort Santiago served as the main defense fortress of the Spanish East Indies, housing troops, armaments, and serving as a strategic stronghold within the walls of Intramuros.
  • Late 19th Century: The fort experienced a period of neglect and decay as the Spanish colonial government focused more on other areas of defense.
  • 1898: During the Spanish-American War, the United States Navy bombarded and captured Manila on August 13. Fort Santiago fell to the American forces, effectively ending Spanish rule in the Philippines.
  • 1899-1941: Fort Santiago became a military headquarters and prison during the American colonial period and the early years of the Commonwealth of the Philippines.
  • 1942-1945: Fort Santiago was used by the Japanese Imperial Army as a prison and torture chamber during World War II. Countless Filipinos and foreign nationals were imprisoned, tortured, and executed within its walls.
  • 1945: The fort was recaptured by American forces in the Battle of Manila, and the Japanese occupation of the Philippines came to an end.
  • 1950s-1970s: Fort Santiago underwent restoration and preservation efforts to turn it into a historical and cultural landmark.

San Agustin Church,

is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the oldest stone church in the Philippines. It was built in 1589 and has survived fires, earthquakes, and wars.

  • 1571: The Spanish conquistador Miguel López de Legazpi established the city of Manila and the Spanish settlement of Intramuros. As part of the effort to spread Christianity, a temporary bamboo and nipa palm church was built on the site of what would later become San Agustin Church.
  • 1586: Due to the vulnerability of the original wooden church to fires, it was decided to construct a more permanent structure using stone.
  • 1595: Construction of the stone church began under the supervision of Spanish friars from the Order of St. Augustine (Augustinians), who were responsible for its design and construction.
  • 1604: The construction of the stone church was completed, and San Agustin Church was officially consecrated. It was dedicated to St. Augustine, the patron saint of the Augustinian Order.
  • Late 1600s to Early 1700s: Additional improvements and enhancements were made to the church, including the addition of elaborate Baroque-style decorations, altars, and religious artifacts.
  • 1754-1755: A major earthquake struck Manila, causing severe damage to many buildings, including San Agustin Church. As a result, the church underwent reconstruction and renovations.
  • 1762: During the British occupation of Manila, San Agustin Church was used as a temporary barracks for British troops.
  • 1854: Another strong earthquake hit Manila, causing significant damage to the church. Reconstruction efforts were initiated to restore the church to its former glory.
  • 1898: The Philippines declared independence from Spanish rule, and San Agustin Church became a symbol of the country's religious and historical heritage.
  • 1945: San Agustin Church survived the devastation of World War II during the Battle of Manila, although it sustained some damage from bombings and artillery attacks in the surrounding area.
  • 1993: San Agustin Church, along with three other Baroque churches in the Philippines, was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, collectively known as the "Baroque Churches of the Philippines."

Paoay Church,

Paoay Church, also known as the Church of San Agustin, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Built in 1694, it is renowned for its distinct Baroque architecture and earthquake-resistant design. Over the years, Paoay Church has faced numerous challenges, including earthquakes, typhoons, and wars. Despite these difficulties, the church has managed to survive and retain its original structure. Restoration efforts have been made to preserve and maintain the church's historical and architectural significance. Paoay Church serves as a symbol of the Spanish colonial era in the Philippines and highlights the country's deep-rooted Catholic faith. It is not only a religious site but also an important cultural and historical landmark that showcases the architectural mastery of the past.

  • 1694: Construction of Paoay Church began under the supervision of Augustinian friars. The church was built using locally sourced materials, including coral stones, bricks, and lumber.
  • 1704: The construction of the church was completed, and it was officially dedicated to St. Augustine, the patron saint of the Augustinian Order.
  • 1865: Paoay Church underwent significant restoration and renovation to reinforce its structure and preserve its historical and architectural value.
  • 1885: Another restoration effort was carried out to repair damages caused by natural elements and to ensure the church's longevity.
  • 1898: The Philippines declared independence from Spanish colonial rule, and Paoay Church became a symbol of the country's religious and historical heritage.
  • 1983: Paoay Church was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List as part of the "Baroque Churches of the Philippines," a group of four churches recognized for their exceptional examples of colonial architecture.
  • 1990s: Paoay Church underwent further restoration and conservation work to protect it from decay and maintain its original features.
  • 2013: Paoay Church, along with other baroque churches in the Philippines, was designated as a National Cultural Treasure by the Philippine government.

The Banaue Rice Terraces ,

are ancient terraces carved into the mountainside by indigenous Ifugao people. They were built over 2,000 years ago and are considered a marvel of engineering and agricultural ingenuity. In 1995, the Banaue Rice Terraces were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site under the title "Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras." This recognition emphasized their outstanding cultural value and the need for their preservation. The UNESCO designation brings global attention and promotes efforts to safeguard this cultural treasure. also known as Luneta Park, is a significant historical site dedicated to the national hero, Jose Rizal. It is where Rizal was executed by the Spanish colonial authorities in 1896.

  • 2,000 to 6,000 years ago: Some archaeological evidence suggests that the ancestors of the Ifugao people, the indigenous group responsible for building the Banaue Rice Terraces, began to cultivate the mountain slopes using stone tools. Gradually, they developed a system of terraced rice fields to accommodate the mountainous terrain.
  • Pre-colonial era: The Ifugao people continued to refine and expand the rice terraces, passing down their knowledge and techniques through generations. These terraces allowed them to grow rice, their staple crop, in the otherwise steep and rugged landscape.
  • 16th to 19th century: During the Spanish colonial period, the Ifugao people resisted attempts at colonization, and their isolation helped preserve their unique culture and traditional farming practices, including the maintenance and expansion of the rice terraces.
  • 20th century: As the Philippines transitioned to American colonial rule, there was increasing pressure on indigenous communities, including the Ifugao, to adopt modern agricultural methods. Some of the terraces fell into disuse during this period.
  • 1960s: Recognizing the cultural and historical significance of the Banaue Rice Terraces, the Philippine government and various organizations started efforts to preserve and protect the terraces and the traditional agricultural practices of the Ifugao people.
  • 1995: The Banaue Rice Terraces were designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing their outstanding cultural value and unique engineering and agricultural techniques.
  • Present: The Banaue Rice Terraces continue to be cultivated and maintained by the Ifugao people, who use traditional methods passed down through generations. The terraces remain a symbol of their cultural identity and a testament to the sustainable land-use practices of the indigenous communities.

Magellan's Cross,

is a Christian cross planted by the Portuguese explorer Ferdinand Magellan in 1521. It marks the arrival of Christianity in the Philippines and is housed in a chapel near Cebu City Hall.

  • 1521: On March 16, the Portuguese explorer Ferdinand Magellan, sailing under the Spanish flag, arrived in the Philippines. He and his crew landed on the island of Homonhon in the eastern part of the archipelago.
  • April 7, 1521: Magellan and his crew reached Cebu, where they were warmly received by Rajah Humabon, the ruler of the island.
  • April 14, 1521: Magellan and his expedition party celebrated the first Catholic mass in the Philippines on the shores of Cebu. Rajah Humabon and some of his followers were baptized, marking the beginning of Christianity in the country.
  • April 21, 1521: Magellan was killed during the Battle of Mactan, a neighboring island of Cebu, in a conflict with the indigenous leader Lapu-Lapu.
  • Late 16th Century: To commemorate Magellan's arrival and the introduction of Christianity, Spanish and Filipino Christians erected a wooden cross on the spot where the first mass was held. This cross came to be known as Magellan's Cross.
  • 1735: The original wooden cross was encased in tindalo wood to protect it from deterioration and damage. The encased cross remains the centerpiece of the chapel that houses Magellan's Cross to this day.
  • Early 1800s: The chapel containing Magellan's Cross was constructed to provide a proper shelter for the sacred relic and to honor its historical and religious significance.
  • Present: Magellan's Cross is a popular tourist attraction in Cebu City and a revered symbol of the country's Christian heritage and colonial history. It is an essential stop for both pilgrims and visitors who want to learn about the Philippines' early encounter with European explorers and the spread of Christianity in the archipelago.

Taal Volcano,

Taal Volcano is one of a sequence of volcanoes that developed on the island of Luzon around 500,000 years ago when two tectonic plates collided. Following the construction of this huge caldera (Taal Lake), additional eruptions built Volcano Island, a volcanic island within Taal Lake. Taal has seen about thirty eruptions since the 16th century, most of which were tiny and limited to Volcano Island. However, sporadic violent action has impacted the whole region, with over 5000 people killed. Taal Volcano was classified a Decade Volcano because to its closeness to inhabited regions and eruptive history.

  • Prehistoric Times: Taal Volcano is considered one of the country's youngest volcanoes and has a long history of eruptions dating back thousands of years. The volcanic island was formed through a series of eruptions, and its main crater eventually filled with water, forming a lake known as Taal Lake.
  • 1572: The first recorded historical eruption of Taal Volcano was documented in 1572. This eruption was witnessed and described by Spanish friars who were present in the Philippines during the Spanish colonial period.
  • 1754: Another significant eruption occurred in 1754, which resulted in the formation of a new island within the caldera lake. This island became known as Volcano Island, which is the current active vent of Taal Volcano.
  • 1911: Taal Volcano had a particularly violent eruption in January 1911, which claimed the lives of more than a thousand people and caused widespread destruction in nearby towns and communities.
  • 1965: Taal Volcano had a phreatic eruption in 1965, characterized by the release of steam and other volcanic gases. This eruption caused minor damage but served as a reminder of the volcano's continued activity.
  • 1991: Taal Volcano experienced a series of phreatic eruptions in 1991, prompting the evacuation of nearby residents and causing concern for potential larger eruptions.
  • 2020: On January 12, 2020, Taal Volcano had a phreatomagmatic eruption, characterized by explosive eruptions caused by the interaction of magma with water. The eruption generated a tall ash column and forced thousands of people to evacuate from the surrounding areas. The Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS) raised the alert status to level 4, indicating a hazardous explosive eruption was possible.
  • Present: Taal Volcano remains an active and closely monitored volcano. PHIVOLCS continues to closely observe and provide updates on the volcano's activity to ensure the safety of nearby communities.

The palace,

was erected in 1750 as a private vacation residence for a Spanish aristocracy named Don Luis Rocha. It was bought by the state in 1825, but it did not become the seat of government until the official mansion of the Spanish governor-general was damaged in an earthquake in 1863. When the Philippines fell under US authority in 1898, it continued to host colonial governors-general before becoming the official seat of Filipino presidents after the nation earned complete independence in the 1940s. Since it became the ruler's residence, the palace has undergone significant extension and renovation. The most substantial renovations, both inside and out, occurred during Ferdinand and Imelda Marcos' occupancy in the 1970s. When Marcos was deposed in 1986, demonstrators temporarily took the palace. With the exception of Gloria Macapagal Arroyo, most presidents have lived in a modest residence on the grounds of Malacanang Palace since then.

  • 1750s: The original structure of Malacañang Palace was constructed by a Spanish aristocrat, Don Luis Rocha, as his riverside mansion.
  • 1802: The Spanish colonial government acquired the property and turned it into the residence of Spanish governors-general in the Philippines.
  • 1863: A major renovation and expansion of the palace were initiated during the administration of Governor-General Rafael de Echague, giving it its present-day neoclassical architectural style.
  • 1898: The Philippines declared independence from Spanish rule, and Malacañang Palace became the residence of the first President of the Philippine Revolutionary Government, General Emilio Aguinaldo.
  • 1901: After the Philippines fell under American colonial rule, Malacañang Palace became the official residence of the American governors-general.
  • 1935: The Philippines became a self-governing Commonwealth under the United States, and Malacañang Palace continued to serve as the official residence of the President of the Commonwealth of the Philippines.
  • 1946: The Philippines gained independence from the United States, and Malacañang Palace became the official residence of the President of the Republic of the Philippines.
  • 1973: During the Marcos era, the palace underwent extensive renovation and expansion under the administration of President Ferdinand Marcos.
  • 1986: Following the People Power Revolution, President Ferdinand Marcos and his family were forced to leave the country, and Corazon Aquino became the first female president of the Philippines. Malacañang Palace remained the official residence of the president.
  • Present: Malacañang Palace continues to serve as the official residence and workplace of the President of the Philippines. It is also the venue for official functions, ceremonies, and state events.

Mount Samat National Shrine (Tagalog pronunciation: [samat]) or Dambana ng Kagitingan (Shrine of Valor),

is a historical shrine located near the summit of Mount Samat in the town of Pilar, Province of Bataan, in the Republic of the Philippines. The memorial shrine complex was built to honor and remember the gallantry of Filipino and American soldiers who fought against the Imperial Japanese Army during World War II.

  • 1942: The Bataan Peninsula became the site of intense fighting during World War II, as Filipino and American soldiers fought against the Japanese Imperial Army.
  • April 9, 1942: Major General Edward P. King Jr., the commanding officer of the USAFFE (United States Army Forces in the Far East) on Bataan, surrendered to the Japanese forces. This event marked the beginning of the Bataan Death March, during which thousands of Filipino and American prisoners of war were forced to march to distant prison camps, resulting in numerous deaths due to harsh conditions and atrocities.
  • April 9, 1966: The construction of the Mount Samat National Shrine began on Mount Samat, a prominent hill overlooking the Bataan Peninsula. The shrine was envisioned to honor the bravery and sacrifice of the Filipino and American soldiers who fought and died in the Battle of Bataan.
  • 1970: The Mount Samat National Shrine was inaugurated and opened to the public. The centerpiece of the shrine is the Dambana ng Kagitingan (Shrine of Valor), a towering white cross standing at around 92 meters (302 feet) tall.
  • 1991: The Shrine of Valor underwent renovations and improvements to preserve its structural integrity and ensure its continued importance as a historical site and memorial.
  • Present: The Mount Samat National Shrine remains an important pilgrimage site for Filipinos and Americans alike. It serves as a place of remembrance and reverence, honoring the heroism and sacrifice of the soldiers who fought during the Battle of Bataan.

The Cagsawa Ruins,

are the remnants of the Cagsawa Church, which was built by Franciscan friars in the 18th century. The church was destroyed during the eruption of Mayon Volcano in 1814, a catastrophic event that buried the town of Cagsawa under volcanic ash. The ruins serve as a reminder of this tragic event and the impact of natural disasters on the region.

  • 1565: The Cagsawa settlement was established by Franciscan friars, who constructed a church made of bamboo and thatch. The church was dedicated to St. John the Baptist.
  • 1595: The construction of the Cagsawa Church was completed using more durable materials, such as stone and bricks. The new church was larger and more substantial than the original bamboo structure.
  • February 1, 1616: A massive eruption of Mayon Volcano buried the Cagsawa Church and the surrounding settlement under thick layers of volcanic debris and lahar, a volcanic mudflow. The eruption claimed the lives of many residents and destroyed several nearby towns.
  • 1724: The Cagsawa Ruins were rediscovered by Spanish friars, who found the remains of the church partially exposed by the eroded volcanic deposits. They also unearthed the church's large bell, known as the "Cagsawa Bell," which is now displayed near the ruins.
  • 1814: Mayon Volcano erupted again, covering the Cagsawa Ruins and the nearby town of Cagsawa with volcanic ash and mud. The eruption prompted the local population to relocate to a safer location, which is the present-day municipality of Daraga.
  • 1934: The Cagsawa Ruins were declared a national park by the Philippine government, recognizing their historical and cultural significance.
  • Present: The Cagsawa Ruins are now a popular tourist destination and a symbol of the destructive power of Mayon Volcano. Visitors come to admire the picturesque view of the ruins against the backdrop of the majestic Mayon Volcano.

Corregidor Island

is located at the entrance of Manila Bay and served as a strategic defense during World War II. It witnessed intense battles between Filipino and American forces against the Japanese. Most people assume that Corregidor is a part of Bataan because of its close physical proximity and historical ties, but it is in fact under the jurisdiction of Cavite City.

  • Late 1500s: The Spanish began to fortify Corregidor Island to defend Manila Bay from pirates and foreign invaders. A lighthouse was also built to aid navigation.
  • 1898: During the Spanish-American War, the United States Navy under Commodore George Dewey defeated the Spanish fleet in the Battle of Manila Bay. After the victory, the Americans established control over Corregidor and the Philippines.
  • 1902: The United States Army Corps of Engineers began the construction of Fort Mills, a coastal defense system, on Corregidor Island.
  • 1941: With the outbreak of World War II, Corregidor Island became a crucial part of the Philippines' defense against Japanese invasion. It served as the headquarters of the Philippine and United States military forces, including General Douglas MacArthur.
  • May 6, 1942: After the fall of Bataan, Corregidor Island surrendered to Japanese forces, marking the end of the Philippine-American defense of the island. This event is known as the Fall of Corregidor.
  • 1942-1945: Corregidor Island remained under Japanese occupation throughout most of World War II. It served as a prison camp for Allied prisoners of war, who endured harsh conditions and forced labor.
  • February 1945: The American forces launched a major offensive to recapture Corregidor Island, known as Operation Topside. After weeks of intense fighting, the island was liberated on March 2, 1945.
  • Present: Corregidor Island is now a popular tourist destination and a historical landmark. It is home to several war memorials and ruins of military installations, serving as a reminder of its strategic importance during World War II.

Manila Cathedral

is recognized as the main Roman Catholic Church in the Philippines. Located in the Plaza de Roma, Intramuros, its official name is Manila Metropolitan Cathedral-Basilica and is the seat of the Archbishop of the Archdiocese of Manila. The western church architecture came in the Philippines with Catholicism around 1521. The Manila Cathedral is one of the most prominent and majestic cathedrals erected during Spanish colonialism. The first church was erected in 1571 by Juan de Vivero, a nonreligious priest. The humble parish of bamboo and nipa was known as the Church of Our Lady of the Immaculate, and its patron was La Purisima Inmaculada Concepcion. Bishop Domingo Salazar transformed the church into a cathedral on December 21, 1581, reconstructing the structure using nipa, bamboo, and wood. However, in 1582, a hurricane hit and ruined the structure. The cathedral was entirely destroyed in a fire a year later. The second church was then constructed between 1591 and 1592. The materials utilized this time were stones. Unfortunately, an earthquake occurred in 1600, causing entire destruction to the church. It was then renovated and completed in 1614. Seven years later, in 1621, another earthquake struck the area, weakening the church's construction. Two powerful earthquakes in late 1625 entirely demolished the cathedral. Archbishop de Poblete oversaw the construction of the fourth church from 1654 until 1751. Unfortunately, by the early 1700s, the structure was in poor condition, and it was decided that further repair was required. Unluckily, a massive earthquake occurred in 1863, destroying the cathedral and its nearby structures. In 1879, the new Manila Cathedral was inaugurated, but another quake happened in 1880, losing the bell tower. In 1945, the cathedral was again ruined due to bombings during the Battle of Manila. It was later reconstructed by Fernando Ocampo from 1954 to 1958 and was introduced in 1958.

  • 1571: The first cathedral was built in Manila after the Spanish conquistador Miguel López de Legazpi established the city. The original cathedral was made of bamboo and nipa palm.
  • 1581: The first stone cathedral was constructed under the supervision of Domingo de Salazar, the first bishop of Manila. The cathedral was made of stone and was dedicated to the Immaculate Conception.
  • 1583: The newly built stone cathedral was damaged by a fire. Reconstruction efforts were initiated immediately to repair and expand the church.
  • 1591: The cathedral was reconstructed and completed in its present form. It was made of adobe and featured a cruciform design with a dome.
  • 1762: The British forces occupied Manila during the Seven Years' War. The cathedral was looted and severely damaged by the invading troops.
  • 1852: A typhoon caused significant damage to the cathedral. Reconstruction efforts were undertaken to repair and strengthen the structure.
  • 1862: The cathedral was solemnly consecrated and officially dedicated to the Immaculate Conception.
  • 1945: During World War II, the Battle of Manila resulted in the destruction of the cathedral. The entire structure, including its historical treasures and artifacts, was reduced to rubble.
  • 1958: Reconstruction of the Manila Cathedral began under the supervision of architect Fernando H. Ocampo. The new cathedral retained its cruciform design and iconic dome.
  • 1958-1959: The reconstructed cathedral was consecrated and blessed in December 1958. The formal dedication took place in April 1959, during the 33rd International Eucharistic Congress held in Manila.
  • 2012-2014: The Manila Cathedral underwent a major renovation and restoration to preserve its historical and cultural heritage and strengthen its structure.
  • Present: The Manila Cathedral remains an important religious and cultural landmark in the Philippines. It serves as the seat of the Archdiocese of Manila and a significant venue for major religious events and celebrations.

  • 1898: On September 15, the Malolos Congress, the revolutionary congress of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule, convened at Barasoain Church. During this historic event, the congress drafted and adopted the Malolos Constitution, which established the First Philippine Republic and declared the Philippines as an independent nation.
  • 1899: The Philippine-American War began, and Barasoain Church served as a hospital and headquarters for both Filipino and American forces at different times during the conflict.
  • 1930: Barasoain Church was declared a National Shrine by the Philippine government, recognizing its historical and cultural significance.
  • 1963: The government of the Republic of the Philippines declared Barasoain Church as a National Historical Landmark.
  • 1998: On September 15, the centennial anniversary of the Malolos Congress was commemorated at Barasoain Church with various celebrations and events.
  • Present: Barasoain Church remains an important historical and religious landmark in the Philippines. It is a popular destination for tourists and visitors interested in the country's history and its struggle for independence.

The Aguinaldo Shrine

is the ancestral home of General Emilio Aguinaldo, the first President of the Philippines and the leader of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. The shrine played a significant role during the Philippine Revolution and the proclamation of Philippine independence in 1898, making it a site of historical importance.

  • 1869: Emilio Aguinaldo was born in Cavite Viejo (now Kawit), Cavite, in the ancestral home of his mother's side of the family.
  • 1896: The Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule began. Emilio Aguinaldo played a prominent role in the revolution, leading Filipino forces in several key battles.
  • 1898: On June 12, 1898, Emilio Aguinaldo declared Philippine independence from Spanish rule in Kawit, Cavite. He also proclaimed himself as the first President of the Philippines and raised the Philippine flag for the first time. The declaration of independence is now known as the Philippine Declaration of Independence.
  • 1923: Emilio Aguinaldo, upon his return from exile, began the reconstruction and restoration of his ancestral home in Kawit, Cavite.
  • 1935: The reconstructed ancestral home of Emilio Aguinaldo was completed, and it became known as the Aguinaldo Shrine.
  • 1964: The Philippine government declared the Aguinaldo Shrine as a National Shrine, recognizing its historical significance.
  • 1963-1965: Aguinaldo's descendants and the Philippine government converted the Aguinaldo Shrine into a museum, showcasing historical artifacts, documents, and personal belongings of General Emilio Aguinaldo and other revolutionary figures.
  • 1998: The Aguinaldo Shrine underwent further renovations and improvements in preparation for the centennial anniversary of the Philippine Declaration of Independence.
  • Present: The Aguinaldo Shrine and Museum is open to the public as a historical and cultural landmark, offering visitors a glimpse into the life and times of General Emilio Aguinaldo and the struggle for Philippine independence.
