Visayas:

Area: 71,503 km2 (27,607 sq mi)

Info: The Visayas is a group of islands in the central part of the Philippines. It includes the major islands of Panay, Negros, Cebu, Bohol, Leyte, and Samar, as well as many smaller islands. The Visayas region is estimated to have around 2,000 islands.

Basilica Minore del Santo Niño

  • Address
  • History
  • Timeline

Osmeña Blvd, Cebu City


Cartesian Coordinates:

10.2947° N, 123.9026° E

The church,

known as Cebu's oldest Catholic Church, holds the image of the Sto. Nino, the patron saint of the city, symbolizes Jesus as a black Holy Child. Every year, a large number of people visit for prayer and pilgrimage. The church has long served as the sanctuary of Santo Nino. The Augustinians were awarded possession of the nipa residence where the Santo Nino was discovered, as well as the surrounding property, on which they built the Philippines' first chapel. The current church was completed in 1739 after four renovations. By 1965, Pope Paul VI had renamed the church Basilica Minore. The church's front mixes Muslim, Romanesque, and neo-classical features in a traditional setting.

  • 1565: Spanish explorers led by Miguel López de Legazpi arrived in Cebu and established a settlement. They presented the image of the Santo Niño to Rajah Humabon's wife, Queen Juana, as a gift to mark their baptism to Christianity.
  • April 28, 1565: The first church on the site, made of bamboo and nipa palm, was built by Spanish friars under the Augustinian order. It was constructed near the spot where the image of the Santo Niño was found.
  • 1566: The first mass in the Philippines was celebrated in the newly built church, making it the oldest Christian church in the country.
  • 1602: A fire destroyed the original bamboo and palm church. A new church, this time made of stronger materials, was constructed to replace it.
  • 1735: Another devastating fire damaged the church. It was reconstructed once again, this time with more durable materials like coral stones, bricks, and tiles.
  • 1965: Pope Paul VI declared the church a Minor Basilica during his visit to the Philippines, recognizing its historical and religious significance.
  • 2013: The Basilica Minore del Santo Niño was elevated to the status of "Mother and Head of All Churches in the Philippines" by the Catholic Bishops' Conference of the Philippines (CBCP), in celebration of the 450th anniversary of the arrival of the Santo Niño image in the Philippines.
  • Present: The Basilica Minore del Santo Niño continues to be a prominent religious and cultural landmark in the Philippines. It is a significant pilgrimage site, attracting millions of devotees and tourists, especially during the annual Sinulog Festival held in honor of the Santo Niño.

Plaza Independencia

M.J. Cuenco Avenue and Legaspi Extension, Barangay San Roque, Cebu City


Cartesian Coordinates:

10.2932° N, 123.9051° E

Plaza Independencia

is a historic public square in Cebu City. It played a significant role in the country's history, witnessing events such as the Battle of Cebu during the Philippine Revolution. Plaza Independencia is located in Cebu City and holds significant historical value. It served as a key site during the Spanish colonial period and played a role in the country's struggle for independence. The plaza witnessed important events, including the signing of the Act of Independence of the Philippines by General Emilio Aguinaldo on December 1, 1898, which declared the country's independence from Spain..

  • Late 16th century: Plaza Independencia was originally established during the Spanish colonial period as "Plaza de Armas" or "Plaza Mayor." It served as a central space for military drills and ceremonies.
  • Late 19th century: The square was renamed "Plaza Libertad" to commemorate the abolition of slavery in the Philippines in 1888.
  • 1901: After the United States took control of the Philippines following the Spanish-American War, Plaza Libertad was again renamed, this time as "Plaza McKinley," in honor of U.S. President William McKinley.
  • 1946: With the granting of Philippine independence from the United States, the plaza was renamed once more, this time as "Plaza Independencia," to symbolize the country's newfound freedom.
  • 1970s: The historic Fort San Pedro, which is adjacent to Plaza Independencia, was restored and opened to the public as a historical park.
  • Present: Plaza Independencia remains a significant historical and cultural landmark in Cebu City. It has been developed into a public park, and the area around it has been preserved to showcase its rich heritage.

Dauis Church,

also known as the Church of Our Lady of the Assumption, is located in Dauis, a small peaceful town from the Island of Panglao. It was declared as a National Historical Landmark by the National Historical Commission. Dauis Church was built in 1697 by the first Spanish missionaries.

  • Early 18th century: The construction of Dauis Church began under the Spanish colonial administration. The church was made of coral stones, a prevalent building material in the region.
  • 1768: The construction of Dauis Church was completed, and it was officially dedicated to Our Lady of the Assumption, the patron saint of Dauis.
  • 19th century: Dauis Church underwent several renovations and enhancements, adding Baroque and neoclassical architectural elements to the original structure.
  • April 8, 2013: A 7.2-magnitude earthquake struck Bohol, causing significant damage to Dauis Church and other heritage structures in the area. The earthquake resulted in the partial collapse of the church's bell tower and inflicted severe structural damage.
  • Present: In the aftermath of the 2013 earthquake, extensive restoration and reconstruction efforts were undertaken to preserve and repair Dauis Church. It remains an essential historical and religious landmark in the Philippines, attracting visitors and pilgrims alike.

The MacArthur Landing Memorial National Park

is located in the central Philippines' east. It is located in the municipality of Palo in the province of Leyte. The national park is one of the world's smallest, with an area of about 0.026 square miles (0.067 square kilometers). The park is not in the conventional natural global national park category, but its cultural and historical importance merits it a position in the world's national park system. General Douglas MacArthur would arrive here on October 20, 1944, to begin the liberation of the Philippines from the Japanese during World War II. This historic landing acted as the impetus for the most catastrophic naval engagement in World War II, with the most ships lost across all fleets. It was the war's largest naval fight.

  • October 20, 1944: During the Battle of Leyte in World War II, General Douglas MacArthur fulfilled his famous promise to return to the Philippines. He led the Allied forces in the amphibious landing on Leyte's shores, specifically at Red Beach in Palo.
  • 1944-1945: The park's construction and development began shortly after the landing, as a tribute to the historic event and as a memorial to the soldiers who fought and died during the Battle of Leyte.
  • 1947: The MacArthur Landing Memorial was officially inaugurated and opened to the public as a national park.
  • 1977: The Philippine government designated the MacArthur Landing Memorial as a national historical landmark, recognizing its historical and cultural significance.
  • 1994: The park underwent renovations and improvements to enhance its facilities and preserve its historical value.
  • Present: The MacArthur Landing Memorial National Park remains an important historical site in the Philippines, attracting both locals and tourists interested in World War II history and General Douglas MacArthur's role in the country's liberation.

The Mactan Shrine

A historical and cultural monument, the Mactan Shrine is located in Lapu-Lapu City, Cebu, Philippines. It commemorates the Battle of Mactan, which took place on April 27, 1521, and is remembered as a pivotal moment in Philippine history. The war symbolized Lapu-Lapu's opposition to Ferdinand Magellan's forces as a native ruler.

  • April 27, 1521: The Battle of Mactan takes place between the forces of native chieftain Lapu-Lapu and the Spanish expedition led by Ferdinand Magellan. Magellan is killed during the battle.
  • 1960s: The idea to create a monument to honor Lapu-Lapu and commemorate the Battle of Mactan gains traction.
  • 1964:Construction of the Lapu-Lapu Monument and the Mactan Shrine begins.
  • 1970: The Lapu-Lapu Monument is completed and stands as a symbol of Filipino resistance against colonization.
  • April 27,1972: The Lapu-Lapu Monument and the Mactan Shrine are officially inaugurated on the 451st anniversary of the Battle of Mactan. The monument is unveiled as a tribute to Lapu-Lapu's heroism and as a symbol of national pride.
  • 1981: The Lapu-Lapu Monument and the Mactan Shrine are declared National Cultural Treasures by the National Museum of the Philippines, recognizing their historical and cultural significance.

Fort San Pedro

In Spanish, the Fort San Pedro (or Fuerte de San Pedro) is a military fortification in Cebu. Under the leadership of Miguel Lopez de Legazpi, the first captain general of the Philippines, the Spaniards constructed it. The first fort, constructed as soon as Legaspi and his expedition arrived, was composed of wood. In order to fend off Muslim raiders, it was later replaced in the 17th century by a stone fort.

  • 1565: Fort San Pedro was established by Spanish conquistador Miguel López de Legazpi and his forces upon their arrival in Cebu. It was originally built as a wooden fort with watchtowers.
  • 1738: Construction of a stone fort begins under the orders of Spanish Governor-General Fernando Valdés y Tamón. The purpose of the stone fort was to protect the Spanish settlement from pirate attacks and potential uprisings.
  • Early 19th Century: Fort San Pedro is used as a stronghold by Filipino revolutionaries during the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule.
  • Late 19th Century: The fort is transformed into a prison and detention center for local insurgents during the Philippine-American War.
  • World War II: The Japanese Imperial Army occupies Fort San Pedro during World War II, using it as a military installation.
  • 1945: American forces liberate Cebu and regain control of Fort San Pedro.
  • 1968: The National Historical Institute (now the National Historical Commission of the Philippines) takes over the administration of Fort San Pedro, recognizing its historical significance.
  • 1970s: Restoration and preservation efforts are initiated to restore Fort San Pedro to its original condition as a historical landmark.
  • Present: Fort San Pedro is now a historical park and museum, showcasing artifacts, exhibits, and information about Cebu's history, Spanish colonial period, and the fort's role in various historical events.

Negros Museum

The Negros Museum originally began construction in 1987 in the Provisional Capitol Building, which was opened on March 16, 1996. The museum was packed with modern contemporary works by local artists that depicted the stories detailing the Negros Islands and the events that took place throughout their eras in addition to the antique old antiques. Through figments of their masterpieces that they sell to the museum, the creative craftspeople of the city give life to the actual and fictional stories they have concocted.

  • 1986: The Negros Museum is established as a cultural institution to promote and preserve the heritage of Negros Occidental.
  • 1996: The museum is formally inaugurated and opens its doors to the public, featuring exhibits that highlight the province's history, art, and cultural diversity.
  • 2004: The Negros Museum undergoes renovations and expansions to improve its facilities and provide more space for exhibits.
  • Present: The museum continues to serve as an important cultural institution, hosting various exhibitions, programs, and events that celebrate the heritage and contributions of Negros Occidental to Philippine history and culture.

Miagao Church

is located in the municipality of Miagao in the province of Iloilo and is one of the most visited sites in the region. This ancient church was built in 1786 and incorporates baroque design elements as well as themes of Chinese and Filipino components in its front, making it a unique place of worship and one of the Philippines' most stunning architectural gemstones. St. Christopher, for example, wears a traditional Filipino dress and is surrounded by decorations such as papaya and palm trees, which are very common in the country. The distinctive yellow-ochre color of the Miagao Church comes from the introduction of coral, ground into dust, and egg whites into the adobe mix. Spaniards typically use egg whites to make mortar for their churches, to make the mixture more robust. In recognition of its numerous architectural styles, it was eventually named a UNESCO World Heritage Site in December 1993.

  • Late 16th Century: Construction of the Miag-ao Church begins around 1787 under the supervision of Spanish friars. The church is built in response to frequent Moro raids and is designed as a fortress-like structure.
  • Early 18th Century: Construction of the church is completed around 1797. The church's unique architecture is a fusion of Baroque, Romanesque, and Gothic styles, with indigenous Filipino influences.
  • 1888: The church is damaged by an earthquake, prompting repairs and restoration efforts.
  • 1993: The Miag-ao Church, along with three other Baroque churches in the Philippines, is designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site under the title "Baroque Churches of the Philippines." This recognition highlights the church's historical and architectural significance.
  • 2000: The church undergoes a major restoration project to preserve and maintain its structural integrity and historical features.
  • Present: The Miag-ao Church remains an active place of worship, attracting both local parishioners and tourists interested in its architectural beauty and cultural importance.
